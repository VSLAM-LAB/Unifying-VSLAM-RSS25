Overview

Are Robotics, particularly Visual SLAM, Tackling the Right Challenges? While fields like computer vision and reinforcement learning have advanced by harnessing vast amounts of unstructured data, SLAM remains trapped in a small set of carefully curated benchmarks. This narrow focus is limiting the development of robust and scalable systems that can reliably operate ‚Äúin the wild‚Äù, i.e., in complex, real-world environments. The root of the issue lies in the fragmentation across SLAM datasets, pipelines, implementations, and evaluation metrics. Each dataset has its own distinct structure‚Äîdiffering in ground-truth formats, data configurations, and calibration methods and models‚Äîmaking benchmarking and reproducibility a significant challenge. Instead of advancing core technologies, researchers are often forced to spend valuable time adapting their developments to different datasets, stifling innovation. In addition, baseline systems are notoriously difficult to compile and integrate, largely due to the absence of standardized pipelines. Dataset-specific issues and implementation failures often go undocumented, leaving the research community to repeatedly address the same problems in isolation. This lack of cohesion is undoubtedly slowing progress in a field that would greatly benefit from streamlined, unified practices. To drive SLAM forward, we must adopt a unified approach across datasets, pipelines, and evaluation metrics. Standardizing dataset formats, establishing common pipelines for baseline integration, and expanding beyond traditional metrics like Absolute Trajectory Error (ATE) and Relative Pose Error (RPE) will enable SLAM to scale and be benchmarked across a wider range of real-world scenarios, including critical factors like uncertainty, safety, and reliability. Only by addressing this fragmentation can the SLAM community shift its focus to solving larger, more impactful challenges and truly leverage the power of large-scale data to achieve the next breakthroughs.

Call for Papers

We invite short papers of novel or recently published research relevant to the topics of the workshop.

  • Short papers are 2+n pages (2 pages content + references)
  • Submissions must follow the IEEE Conference double column format.
  • All accepted papers will be presented as posters at the workshop and published on the workshop website.
  • Please indicate whether your paper falls into the ‚Äònovel‚Äô or ‚Äòpreviously published‚Äô category. Novel research papers are encouraged and can expect more substantial review feedback on their work. This is provided as a service to authors of novel papers and does not diminish the chance of acceptance.
  • All accepted submissions will be considered for the best presentation award, where 3 finalists will be selected for 5-minute plenary presentations. While all submissions are eligible, novelty will be considered in finalist selection.
  • Submissions are single blind and will be reviewed by members of the (extended) workshop committee.
  • Submissions can optionally be accompanied by a video.

Invited Topics

We invite contributions from the areas of:

  • Unification of SLAM Pipelines
  • Innovative Datasets
  • Novel Evaluation Metrics

Review Criteria

Novel submission will be evaluated on:

  • Relevance to the topics of the workshop
  • Recency and novelty
  • Clarity of presentation
  • Technical Quality
  • Strength of results (i.e., the results show promise for early stage work, the (planned) experimental setup is adequate)

Papers that have previously been published will mainly be evaluated on the first two points (relevance and recency). If so, please mention in the submission where it has been published.

Call for Demonstrations

We invite live demonstrations during the poster session. These can either accompany a submitted paper or be standalone. For standalone demonstrations, please submit an up to two pages description and/or video of the demo. Demos will be evaluated on recency and relevance to the workshop.

Provocative Questions We Hope to Discuss

  • How can we establish standardized dataset formats and pipelines to streamline SLAM development and benchmarking across different platforms and research groups?
  • What new evaluation metrics should be introduced to better assess SLAM performance in terms of uncertainty, safety, reliability, and scalability in real-world applications?
  • Is the current reliance on small, curated datasets limiting SLAM's potential, and what strategies can be employed to effectively leverage large-scale, unstructured data in the same way fields like reinforcement learning and computer vision have done?
